Selected references on Group Testing

Yukito Iba (The institute of statistical mathematics)

About this page

The following list provides selected literature on group testing. Most papers in the list are published in pre-corona age; it also includes a few examples of coronavirus testing, but we do not attempt to give the latest information on COVID-19 application. We do not attempt to provide a comprehensive historical review either. All references are found free on the web in August 2020.

Group Testing

There are two types of group testing. The first category, hereafter referred as “Type A”, contains methods that aim at efficient identification of infected individuals using pooled samples. The second category, hereafter referred as “Type B”, interested in the estimation of “macroscopic” quantities, such as prevalence of a disease. If positive samples are rare, grouping adequate number of samples can much improve the efficiency of the estimation.

Type A

Dorfman (1943): The detection of defective members of large populations, Ann. Math. Statist., 14, 436-440.

The original paper of Type A group testing.

Aldridge, Johnson, and Scarlett (2019): Group testing: An information theory perspective, Foundations and Trends in Communications and Information Theory 15, 196-392. arxiv

A comprehensive, well-cited review on theoretical aspects of Type A group testing. In Sec 1.7, applications of group testing is listed, which provide examples other than screening of a disease.

Bilder and Tebbs (2012): Pooled testing procedures for screening high volume clinical specimens in heterogeneous populations, Statistical Medicine, 31, 3261-3268.

A brief introduction from a medical viewpoint. References to various methods of group testing are useful.

Litvak, Tu, and Pagano (1994): Screening for the presence of a disease by pooling sera samples, Journal of the American Statistical Association, 89, 424-434.

An application of multistage group testing to HIV screening is proposed. Sensitivity and specificity of the test are discussed; the importance of the assumption of independence among tests is stressed in Sec 4.

Armstrong, Klein, and Hickey (2020): Evaluation of group testing for SARS-CoV-2 RNA. medRxiv

This paper proposes the use of a matrix testing, while many of the application to SARS-CoV-2 RNA testing seems to use a simple Dorfman procedure. Discussion on the possible difficulties in actual implementation of complex group testing methods is given.

Noam et al. (2020): Efficient high-throughput SARS-CoV-2 testing to detect asymptomatic carriers, Science Advances 21 eabc5961. URL

Another example of complex group testing for SARS-CoV-2, which is implemented in a clinical study.

Type B

Thompson (1962): Estimation of the proportion of vectors in a natural population of insects, Biometrics, 18, 568-578.

One of the pioneering studies on Type B group testing. A simple two-stage test is discussed with maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) and its variance. [ Gibbs and Gower (1960) Annals of Applied Biology 48, 75-83 ] seems published earlier, but I have not seen their paper.

Brookmeyer (1999): Analysis of multistage pooling studies of biological specimens for estimating disease incidence and prevalence, Biometrics, 55, 608-612.

A good introduction on Type B group testing, which discuss multistage tests and their medical applications. It is quite readable, but the derivation of maximum likelihood estimator for multistage tests seems unnecessarily complicated.

Hepworth (1996): Exact confidence intervals for proportions estimated by group testing, Biometrics, 52, 1134-1146.

MLE in Type B group testing is sometimes considerably biased, although multistage method could partially resolve the problem. In Sec 3 of this paper, references on this problem are given, which will be useful for implementation.
